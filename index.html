üìÑ 3. A Conceptual Framework for a Cosmic Time System Based on Photon Travel

This paper proposes a conceptual framework for a universal time system based on the travel of light across the average separation between galaxies. Traditional measures of time, such as days and years, are tied to Earth's motions and lose relevance on cosmic scales. By using photon travel time as a fundamental unit, this framework directly links the passage of time to the expanding universe. The study distinguishes between two perspectives: the instantaneous time calculated based on the current separation of galaxies, and the actual time a photon experiences while traveling as the universe continues to expand. It further explores how observers at different epochs perceive these times differently due to accelerated cosmic expansion. This approach provides a physically motivated measure of cosmic time grounded in fundamental processes and cosmological dynamics. The framework highlights the relative nature of temporal perception across cosmic epochs while offering a potential basis for a practical, scalable measure of time beyond planetary references.

üìÑ 4. Recursive and Inverse Structure of Hyperoperations

This paper explores the recursive and inverse behavior of hyperoperations, a hierarchy that extends exponentiation, tetration, and pentation to ever higher orders. While the structure of hyperoperations has long been known, their recursive formulation and inversion have not always been presented in a unified or general way. In this study, we define a generalized inverse function that extends logarithmic and super-logarithmic concepts across all levels of the hyperoperation hierarchy. We demonstrate that these inverse functions obey elegant recursive and cross-level identities. The derivations are presented from first principles, using a conceptual approach to clarify the behavior of these extremely fast-growing operations.

üìÑ 5. Exploration of All Motion Derivatives

In classical mechanics, motion is typically described through displacement, velocity, and acceleration, with occasional extensions to higher derivatives such as jerk or snap. Beyond these, higher-order derivatives are rarely considered, despite their well-defined mathematical structure. This work explores the complete hierarchy of motion derivatives, extending the familiar framework into an infinite sequence. Each derivative is denoted œàk, where k represents the order of differentiation of position with respect to time. While lower orders correspond to standard physical quantities, higher orders can still be treated systematically as abstract measures of change. We examine both finite and infinite summations and products of these derivatives, revealing connections to Taylor expansions and highlighting convergence challenges. To quantify the overall behavior of motion, we introduce a motion complexity functional, which blends contributions from derivatives of all orders using weighted norms. This measure captures how higher-order variations influence the structure of motion, distinguishing between smooth, uniform trajectories and erratic, rapidly changing ones. Finally, we extend the framework into an infinite-dimensional vector space where each derivative occupies its own axis, enabling a geometric interpretation of motion across all orders. This approach provides a unified, rigorous foundation for analyzing motion complexity, with potential applications in robotics, control systems, and trajectory optimization where higher-order smoothness and precision are critical.
